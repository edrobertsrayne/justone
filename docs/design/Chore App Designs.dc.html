Design proposal · v1

The one-task-a-day chore app

Working from your spec. The product's whole personality is restraint — it shows one thing, nudges a few times, then respects your no. So the design has to feel calm before it feels clever. Below: my reading of the system, three visual directions to react to, a working prototype from onboarding through the daily loop, and every remaining screen.

The sacred path

One card. Swipe right to do, left to skip, long-press to remove. Completing is the only way to advance anything — it lives nowhere but here.

One honest cue

No pips, no counters, no streak number on the daily screen. The card's only ambient signal is warmth — it glows warmer the more overdue the task. Everything else is silence.

Two tiers, one loss

Streak = showing up (≥1). Target = doing well (N). Only the streak can break; the reward only ever accumulates — so the reveal celebrates, never threatens.

Working prototype

Direction A — “Clearing” · live, try it

What this proves out
Swipe physics

Card follows your finger with a slight tilt, snaps back under threshold, flings past it. Hold to remove is a separate, deliberate gesture.

Reactive limits

No reroll counter sits on screen. Skip your last one and the card tells you — only then.

The reveal

Streak appears only at the target moment. “Keep going” is pure upside — the banked streak is never at risk.

The name — Just One

Chosen

Short, ownable, and clearly about getting a task done — while carrying the whole “just one, that's enough” philosophy in two words. It sidesteps the crowded “One Thing” space and the Nike echo of “Just Do One.” This welcome screen is now the live entry point of the prototype above.

Why it works
Says what it does

“One” reads as one task — the single-card mechanic is right there in the name.

Carries the feeling

“Just” softens it — just one, no pressure. The reassurance is built in.

Ownable

Two short words, easy as a wordmark, handle, and URL. Worth a quick trademark + domain check before lock-in.

The one cue, dialled up

Direction A's halo is the whole urgency language — no badges, no “overdue” labels. It stays cool and faint at baseline and warms toward orange as time pressure rises. Time pressure is the only lever; a task with no deadline simply sits at baseline and surfaces on a random draw.

Notifications & sign-in

Pushes nudge you over the floor (your first task), never chase the target. Each fires only if the streak isn't secured; doing one suppresses the rest. Content is opaque — it carries streak status, not the task name — so opening the app is still a reveal. The final beat is the only one that states the real stakes, calmly.
